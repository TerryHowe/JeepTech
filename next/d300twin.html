by Christo Slee

A twin stick shifter for the Dana 300 can be fabricated fairly easily. A twin stick shifter for the Dana 300 will make shifting easier and allow some shift combinations that are not available with the single stick shifter. A twin stick shifter will allow easy shifting into front wheel drive low and rear wheel drive low. A detent on the shift rails prevents front wheel drive high. The best part about the twin stick conversion is it looks cool.

A second stock Dana 300 shift lever is required to do the conversion.

The single flat piece of metal with the two holes that slides into the slot on the shift rails has two problems if you are trying to do this at home and you don't have the right tools.

  1. If it is not machine accurately the rotational forces when pushing lever back (bottom part forward) makes the plate rotate around the pin in the shift rail.
  2. If the thicker part of the new adapter is not think enough the shifter will slip out of the big hole when the above happens. The stock adapter that links the two shiftrails together has the hole about 3/4" above the middle of the shifter rails. The stock hole aligns with the thin part above the "ball" on the bottom of the shifter.

After trying the above and not being very successful I finally did the following.

  1. Drilled the second shifter lever to go over the pivot pin. Mine actually turned out to have too much play after drilling so I used some copper water pipe that happened to be the exact size to act as a bushing in between. You want the shifter to rotate around the pin with the minimum of play. The new shifter goes in between the two tabs that the pin slide into. The old shifter stays in the stock position.
  2. Got two pieces of pipe about 1 1/2" long that slides over the shifter rails with minimum clearance. Kinda like a bushing. By doing this you eliminate any rotation problems of the connecting block.
  3. Drilled a 1/4" hole in each in the center. This is for the pin, I just bought two longer 1/4" cottepins and ground them down a bit to fit.
  4. Made two "tabs" to weld to the side. Drilled each one with a bigger hole to accept the bottom of the shifter. The easiest is to make the bushings first, fit them over the shift rails and position them. Then determine the best position for the tabs, based on the shifter positions etc. You might have to bend the shifters to eliminate interference.
  5. I then welded the tabs to the bushings. The tabs were positioned towards the top side of the bushing and not in the middle. This allows for the bottom of the shifter to stay in the hole and also align better (closer to the stock position).
  6. Assemble everything and check for interference. I had to bend the shifters a little.
