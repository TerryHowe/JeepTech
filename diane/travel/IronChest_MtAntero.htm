Colorado/Central Colorado/Chaffee County/Iron Chest and Mount Antero

Iron Chest and

Mount Antero

by: Diane Howe

 

Mining Structures along Iron Chest Trail

Chaffee County is home to the Upper Arkansas Valley region – a popular destination for outdoor enthusiasts. From the Arkansas River to the many peaks rising above 14,000 feet, outdoor fun at many altitudes can be had on foot, on horseback, in 4-wheel drive vehicles, in a boat, or on your derrier soaking in hot springs.

In the 1860s, mining brought many settlers to this area. Aptly enough, our exploration of this region starts by taking a 4-wheel drive vehicle off-road to Iron Chest mine. According to Guide to Colorado Backroads & 4-Wheel Drive Trails by Charles A. Wells (FunTreks, Inc, 1998), Iron Chest is rated a black diamond, mostly because of the first half mile.Lifts, winches, and lockers are recommended.

The trail is about 2 ½ miles, and travel time really depends on your vehicle set-up.The trail rises to above tree line and features old mines, mining equipment, and structures where the miners used to live.Past the mine, you can continue up Chrysolite Mountain, peaking at 12,822 feet, and get good views.The last several hundred yards to the top of Chrysolite is a hike, since the vehicle trail ends nearby some old mining structures and equipment.

Closeby to Iron Chest is Mount Antero rising to 14,269 feet. You can take a four-wheel drive vehicle very close to the top of Antero, and hike the rest to the peak. This area is popular for bikers, hikers, and 4-wheelers. Along the trail up to Mount Antero, there are several beautiful free (primitive) camping spots and wildlife. The marmots were in abundance above tree-line.
It is better to start early in the day to see these high altitude destinations due to thunderstorms, with lightening, that roll through later in the day. We drove to the end of the vehicle trail below Mount Antero at around 4:30 and decided not to hike the several hundred yards to the top, because of lightning strikes very close to us. Also, the higher altitude trails in Colorado are snow covered for most of the year. Depending on the season, mid-July through early September are your best bets for avoiding snow. During June and late-September you start to push your luck with the weather.

To get to the head of Iron Chest trail, take 285 to 162 west near Nathrop. After 15 miles turn left on Hancock Road 295 then watch for the small parking area on the left after .4 miles.

To get to the trailhead that ascends Mount Antero, from 285, take 162 west near Nathrop approximately 11-12 miles. Across from Alpine reservoir, take a left onto FR 277.
