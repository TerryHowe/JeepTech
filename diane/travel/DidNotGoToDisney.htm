When I didn’t go to Disney

by Diane Howe

 

My family and I had a trip planned to visit Disney World in Florida at the end of September, 2001 and we didn’t go.  It wasn’t because terrorists had just crashed planes into American buildings that represented security, freedom, and capitalism, bringing them down and killing 5600 people doing so, shaking up the world we live, making us afraid and wanting to stay home.  But, rather, it is because my mom collapsed on the floor in the kitchen one evening, 58 years old with a pulse rate of 40, and was rushed to the hospital after a 9-1-1 call.

 

I was to meet my mom and Bernie, sis, and my 3 nieces in Disney for a long-planned vacation get-away.  This was totally my mom’s dream.  She was treating her granddaughters to the fantasy world of Disney, where mice were bigger than people and can talk and get their picture taken with you.  Where parades lead by princesses are a matter of course and Main Street has candy shops and toys and perfectly manicured sidewalks and well kept shops and anything a little girl or boy can dream of.  She was living to see their faces light up when they saw Mickey or Cinderella’s castle or their surprise when they rode some thrilling ride.  She had been planning this since Mary, my six year old niece, was born and, from the reports that I hear, as long as religious fundamentalists had been planning their rides of doom.

 

After September 11, my sister and I had several conversations about whether we should take the trip to Florida at the end of the month like we had planned.   Actually, we knew we wanted to go, but we were dealing with whether it was appropriate or not and how guilty we would feel doing something that would actually be a good time.  Especially, my sister pointed out, how bad she felt about all those children who lost parents in the tragedy and would never even get to go to Disney with them.    I talked about how I try not to let fear stop me and, if we didn’t go, wouldn’t we be doing exactly what the terrorists wanted?  My family was driving to Florida from their homes in Pennsylvania so flying for them wasn’t an issue.  I was flying in from Colorado and had no qualms about doing so figuring security was extreme after the attacks.  We decided to go.

 

The week before I was to meet them in Florida, my mom collapsed in the kitchen where she was getting a midnight snack.  She was diagnosed with diabetes about 10 years ago, and probably had it years before being diagnosed.  Within hours of going to the hospital the doctors operated and gave her a pacemaker in a surprisingly simple operation.  She was released from the hospital on a Tuesday and the doctors recommended that she not leave for Florida on Thursday as planned.   I decided to travel to Pennsylvania and see my mom with the few days I had arranged off from work.

 

Flying at the end of September was a definite different experience than I had ever experienced.  The closest thing was flying in Central America or Europe, where armed guards are already part of the scenery.  The weirdest thing was it was on our own soil.  Before going through airport security, I grabbed a bagel and cream cheese to eat later and wondered if I should put a plastic knife in my purse.  I decided, yes, I’m not going to allow terrorists have me get my fingers sloppy and I put the knife with the bagel in my purse.

 

The X-ray machines at the airport picked up my nail clippers and my bag was searched.  I showed the airport employee where the clippers were and he showed me the metal file that folded out from them.  It never even occurred to me to use something like that as a weapon.  I told him he can keep them, as I tried very hard not to look like a terrorist.  I started getting nervous about the plastic knife, but they didn’t bother with it.

 

I was very alert and looked at everyone on the plane as I found my seat.  During the meal service on the flight, there were only plastic utensils.  Later, I realized that all the food places at the airport had plastic utensils.  Another minor shift from my previous reality.

 

As we were approaching Newark, I remembered that you can see downtown NYC from the airport but I was on the wrong side of the plane to see.  The attacks seemed even closer now that I traversed the miles from Colorado in a few short hours.  The guy across the aisle from me pulled out  a camera and started shooting out the window of the plane.

 

It was a clear day in Newark and I filed into the terminal to the windowed walkway leading to the main terminal.   I had to look several times before realizing that I indeed was viewing the downtown Manhattan skyline.  I kept thinking I was seeing Newark because the scene without the Twin Towers looked so alien.  The gap inside me grew to the size of the gap in the skyline and I started to cry.

 

It used to be that the World Trade Center commanded the lower Manhattan skyline, but not anymore. It used to be that airlines gave proper utensils during flight meals, but not anymore. It used to be that people weren't afraid before going to the Post Office or going to work, but not anymore. It used to be that Mommies seemed strong, would live forever, and stop anything bad from happening, but not anymore.
